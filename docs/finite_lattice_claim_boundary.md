# Finite-Lattice Claim Boundary

This repository is organized around a strong research objective: build a disciplined RQM/QSG route toward the Yang-Mills mass gap while keeping standard Yang-Mills evidence, interpretation, and nonstandard deformations clearly separated.

## What Finite-Lattice Diagnostics Can Contribute

Finite-lattice packets can make the baseline auditable. They record the configuration, observables, diagnostic summaries, correlators, effective-mass estimators, and plots needed to understand a small numerical run.

These diagnostics can help:

- test the standard SU(2) Wilson-action implementation,
- compare local-action and full-action update paths,
- identify suspicious thermalization or autocorrelation behavior,
- compare beta and seed dependence in tiny sanity sweeps,
- look for gauge-invariant closure-resonance candidates in correlator data.

## What They Cannot Establish Alone

Finite-lattice diagnostics are not a continuum construction. They do not by themselves establish the Clay Yang-Mills mass gap.

Additional work is required:

- controlled larger-volume and smaller-spacing studies,
- improved gauge-invariant operators,
- thermalization, autocorrelation, and uncertainty analysis at scale,
- a precise continuum limit,
- a proof target that connects the observed packet behavior to a spectral gap in the continuum theory.

## Safe RQM/QSG Language

Use language such as:

- closure-resonance working hypothesis,
- candidate mechanism,
- finite-lattice diagnostic,
- gauge-invariant correlator signature,
- proof target.

Avoid writing as if the finite-lattice data has already settled the continuum theorem. The role of packets is to sharpen the attack on the problem, not to blur diagnostic evidence into a final result.
